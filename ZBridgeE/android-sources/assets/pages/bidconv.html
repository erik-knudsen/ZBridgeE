Bidding Conventions.

Bidding conventions supported by ZBridge are the following.

Four-Card and Five-Card Majors.

When playing Four-Card majors, any four-card suit can be opened.  When playing Five-Card majors, you can only open a major suit if it has five or more cards.  If you don't have any 5-card major suits, a 3-card minor can be opened instead. See General in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.


Strong 2-Bids.

Strong 2 Bids generally indicate 20+ points in the hand.  A strong 2 Club bid indicates the strongest hand of all, with a minimum ranging from 20 to 23 points (but usually 22). See General in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.


Weak 2-Bids.

Weak 2 Bids are used to open hands that have a good long suit but otherwise lack the strength to open at the 1 level.  Generally the hand will have a 6-card major with 2+ honors, a short side suit, and 6-11 points.
A response of 2NT to an opening weak 2 is forcing and is conventional and could be either Ogust or Feature. See Generals in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.


Artificial 2 Club Opening.

The Artificial 2 club opening indicates a strong hand of about 22+ points. This is a forcing bid; partner must respond positively with his best suit at the cheapest level if slam is a possibility, or negatively at 2D if slam is unlikely.
If partner responds negatively at 2D, opener should bid his best suit.  Partner can then pass or indicate his own best suit if below game.  It is then up to Opener's discretion to return to his suit or support partner's suit.  See General in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.


Weak Jump Overcalls.

A Jump Overcall is a bid made over an opponent at a level one higher than necessary (e.g., an overcall of 2S instead of 1S over an opponent’s 1H opening).  Normally such an overcall of an opponent's bid at the 2-level indicates a strong hand with 15-18 points.  When playing Weak Jump Overcalls, however, the bid indicates a hand similar to a weak 2 opening -- i.e., 6-11 points and a 6+ card suit.
A response of 2NT to a Weak Jump Overcall can be forcing and conventional and could be either Ogust or Feature if it is not natural (preconfigured in standard bid convention sets (SAYC etc.).). See Misc in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.
Note that an overcall at the 3-level or higher is not a jump overcall but rather a preemptive bid.


Stayman Convention.

The Stayman convention is the bid of 2 Clubs in response to partner’s 1NT opening.  The bid asks partner whether he has a 4-card major suit.  If the opener has a 4+ card suit in either Hearts or Spades, he should respond with 2H or 2S, accordingly.  If he has 4 cards in both Hearts and Spades, he should respond in Hearts.  And if he lacks a 4-card major, he should respond negatively with 2D.
Stayman can also be used in response to 2NT openings by bidding 3 Clubs; the responses by opener are then at the 3-level.  See General in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.


Jacoby Transfers.

The Jacoby Transfer convention is used in response to an opening bid of 1NT.  It directs the opener to make a bid in the next higher suit.  Generally, the responder will have a 5-card suit.
For instance, opposite partner’s 1NT opening you might hold five Hearts.  You know that partner has at least two hearts (with a balanced hand), so you want to play in hearts.  But since partner has the stronger hand, you want him to be the declarer.  This is because it has been shown that playing the contract with the stronger hand hidden can provide one extra trick on average.
You therefore make a Jacoby Transfer bid at 2 Diamonds, which instructs partner to bid the next higher suit, or 2 Hearts.  If partner really likes hearts, he can instead bid 3H. See General in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.


Four-Suit Transfers (aka Jacoby Transfers to Minors).

Normally, Jacoby transfers only allow the responder to specify a transfer to Hearts, Spades, or Clubs using bids of 2D through 2S, respectively.  A bid of 2C is reserved for Stayman, and 2NT is a natural raise.
If Four-Suit Transfers are enabled, the bid of 2NT becomes a fourth shift bid, indicating that opener should bid Diamonds.  Preconfigured in standard bid convention sets (SAYC etc.).


Limit Raises.

In the US, a single raise of a suit from the one level to the two level indicates 6 to 9 points, and a double raise to the three level indicates 13-18 points.  This leaves a gap if responder holds 11 to 12 points.  Normally in this case an alternate suit is bid by responder, leaving the opener uncertain about partner’s holdings.
Limit raises address this problem by changing the meaning of a double raise to a holding of 10-12 points with four card support.  Of course, this raises the question of what to do with 13+ pts and good support when playing limit raises – and the usual answer is to shift to another suit.  See General in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.


Takeout Doubles.

A takeout is a double called at a bidder’s first opportunity after the opposition makes a bid.  It indicates that the bidder has opening values with at least supporting strength (generally 3+ cards) in all unbid suits.  For instance, examine the following sequence:

North East South
Pass 1C Double


The above double indicates that South holds at least 12 points along with three or more cards in each of Diamonds, Hearts, and Spades.  This is a forcing bid, and partner is required to respond in his best suit of the three.  However, if the other opponent (West in this case) makes an intervening bid, the takeout is no longer forcing, and partner (North) can pass with a weak hand.  Also, in rare cases partner may pass and convert the takeout into a penalty double with length in the opponents’ suit.
Doubles over 1 and 2-level opening bids are always for takeout. Doubles over 4-level are always for penalty.  See Misc in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.

Negative Doubles.

Negative Doubles are similar to takeout doubles in that they indicate strength in the unbid majors.  Negative doubles, however, are made by responder after partner has opened the bidding and the right-hand opponent has overcalled.  For example, examine the following sequence.

North East South
1C 1D Double


The above double indicates that South has good holdings in each of Hearts and Spades, but does not like Clubs.  She may or may not have good holdings in Diamonds.
Negative doubles can be made on overcalls of up to 2S.  A negative double made on a 1-level overcall indicates 4+ cards in the unbid majors and 6+ points.  A negative double made on a 2-level overcall indicates 5+ cards in the unbid majors and 11+ points.  See Misc in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.


Unusual No Trump.

The Unusual No Trump bid is an overcall of 2NT over an opponent's 1-level opening bid.  It indicates at least 5 cards in each of the two lowest unbid suits.

Examples of Unusual NT Bids and their meanings:

West North Explanation
1C 2NT North promises 5/5 length in Diamonds and Hearts
1D 2NT Promises 5/5 in Clubs and Hearts
1H 2NT Promises 5/5 in Clubs and Diamonds
1S 2NT Again, promises 5/5 in Clubs and Diamonds


The convention is normally used with either a weak hand with 6-11 pts, or a strong hand with 17+ pts.  When an intermediate hand of 12-16 pts is held, a simple overcall or takeout double is preferred.
Partner should always assume that partner made the Unusual NT bid with the weaker type of hand.  He should pick one of the suits and respond at the 3 level if game does not appear likely.  Alternatively, He can raise to game if it appears attainable.  A cue bid of the enemy suit can also be used to invite slam.  See Misc in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.


Michaels Cue Bids.

The Michaels Cue bid is related to the Unusual No Trump bid.  It is the direct 2-level overcall of an opening 1-level suit bid by the immediately previous opponent (RHO), and indicates a 2-suited hand.  When overcalling a minor suit, it indicates 5/5 shape in the majors, or exceptionally, 5 in one major and a strong 4 in the other.  When overcalling a major, it indicates 5/5 in the other major and an unspecified minor.

Examples of Michaels Bids and their meanings:

West North Explanation
1C 2C North promises 5/5 in the majors
1H  2H Promises 5 Spades plus 5 in an unspecified minor
1NT 2NT Not Michaels; the overcall needs to be of a suit.
2D 3D Not Michaels; the overcall needs to be at the 2-level.


As with the Unusual No Trump bid, the convention is normally used with either a weak hand with 6-11 pts, or a strong hand with 17+ pts.  When an intermediate hand of 12-16 pts is held, a simple overcall or takeout double is preferred.
It is up to the responder to pick a suit from among the two 5-card majors or inquire about the minor.
When the two suits are known (i.e., the two majors), partner is free to bid one of the suits.  A response at the 2 level is not forcing; a response at the 3 level is invitational, and at the 4-level is a preemptive shutout bid or sacrifice.  See Misc in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.


Jacoby 2NT.

A Jacoby 2NT is the response of 2NT to an opening 1-level major suit bid by partner.  It shows 12+ points, good trump support, and is forcing to game.
In response to the Jacoby 2NT bid, opener must indicate his strength by making one of the following bids:

Bid Meaning
3 of the bid suit Strong hand, 18+ pts
3 of another suit Shows a medium hand (15-17 pts) with a singleton or void in the specified suit
3NT Medium hand with, balanced with no short suits
4 of another suit Medium hand with a strong 5-card side suit
4 of the bid suit Non of the above apply -- minimum opener, so sign off in game


Partner can then either sign off in game, or push on towards slam. Preconfigured in standard bid convention sets (SAYC etc.).


Gambling 3NT (Standard and ACOL).

The Gambling 3NT is an opening bid of 3NT.  It is usually made with a long, solid minor and little outside strength.

The standard version of the Gambling 3NT is made with a solid 7+ card minor suit, 10-12 HCPs, no voids or small singletons, and no outside stoppers.  If partner has stoppers in at least three suits and no void minors, she can pass.  Otherwise, she should bid 4 Clubs, which opener can correct to 4 Diamonds if necessary.

The ACOL version is much less of a gambling bid.  It is also made with a 7+ card solid minor, no void or small singleton, but with 16-21 HCPs and stoppers in 2 or (usually) 3 outside suits.  In this version, partner should always pass unless she has interest in slam.
Preconfigured in standard bid convention sets (SAYC etc.).


Fourth Suit Forcing.

Normally when a partnership goes through four suits, there is little chance that the pair will agree on the fourth suit as trump.  For this reason, some players prefer to use the bid of a fourth suit as artificial and forcing in order to keep the bidding going. Preconfigured in standard bid convention sets (SAYC etc.).

Blackwood Convention.

The Blackwood convention is used to test for Aces and Kings in preparation for a possible slam.

A bid of 4NT, when not in direct response to an NT bid from partner, is an inquiry of how many Aces partner has.  Partner is obligated to respond using the following codes to indicate his holdings of Aces:

5 Club Zero or all four Aces
5 Diamond One Ace
5 Heart Two Aces
5 Spade Three Aces


If partner responds 5 Club, the player should know from the context (and his own hand) whether partner means zero or all four Aces.

At this point, the player can either bail out at a 5-level contract, if possible, or bid slam, or continue to query partner for more information.  A second round of Blackwood can be used by bidding 5NT, asking for Kings.  The responses from partner parallel the earlier structure:
6 Club Zero or all four Kings
6 Diamond One King
6 Heart Two Kings
6 Spade Three Kings


Since bidding 5NT forces the partnership to commit to a slam, it is generally not done unless the partnership holds all four Aces.  At this point, the bidder can use his best judgment to decide whether to play a small slam or a grand slam, and in what suit. Preconfigured in standard bid convention sets (SAYC etc.).


Gerber Convention.

The Gerber convention is used to test for Aces and Kings in preparation for a possible slam after an NT bid.

Over a natural bid of 1NT or 2NT, a response of 4 Club is a Gerber bid asking for Aces.  Partner must respond using the following codes to indicate his holdings of Aces:

4 Diamond Zero or all four Aces
4 Hearts One Ace
4 Spade Two Aces
4 Notrump Three Aces


If partner responds 4 Diamond, the player should know from the context (and his own hand) whether partner means zero or all four Aces.

At this point, the player can either bail out at a 5-level contract, if possible, or bid slam, or continue to query partner for more information.  A second round of Gerber can be used by bidding 5 Club, asking for Kings.  The responses from partner parallel the earlier structure:
5 Diamond Zero or all four Kings
5 Heart One King
5 Spade Two Kings
5 Notrump Three Kings


If the player that instigated Gerber bids anything other than 5C in the second pass, it is strictly to play.
Preconfigured in standard bid convention sets (SAYC etc.).


Control Bids.

Control Bids are used as a safer alternative to Blackwood to check for slam prospects.  A control bid is a bid of an alternate suit at the 3-level or higher after a trump suit has been agreed upon. It indicates that the bidder holds an Ace in that suit, and asks partner to name the next-cheapest suit that she holds an ace in.  The responder can then signal encouragement by showing the ace, or discourage slam interest by returning to the contract suit. 


Splinter Bids.

A Splinter is a double jump shift in response to a 1-level opening bid by partner.  It indicates the points for a double raise, plus a singleton or void in the suit shifted to.  It indicates strong interest in slam.  Preconfigured in standard bid convention sets (SAYC etc.).ration dialog.


No Trump Opening Ranges.

Most players use a Strong 1 No Trump, signifying a balanced hand with either 15-17 or 16-18 High Card points.  Some players also use a Weak No Trump, often 12-14 points.

Likewise, people don’t always agree on the appropriate point counts for an opening of 2NT or 3NT.  In fact, some people don’t like to open 3NT at all, instead preferring the general purpose 2 strong 2 Club opening.  A 3NT opening is also often reserved for the Gambling 3NT convention.  See Notrump in the Bid and Play Configuration dialog.


Reverse Restrictions.

A Reverse is a shift to a higher ranking suit by opener at the two-level after previously opening at the one-level.  It is commonly accepted that a player making a reverse must have at least 16 points.


3 Suit Responses to 1NT Opening.

There are three options for 3C and 3D responses to 1NT opening bids.

  • Slam Try
    3C/3D hows a good 5-card or longer suit, and promises slam interest.
  • Game Invitation
    3C/3D shows a good 6-card or longer suit, and is invitational to 3NT.
  • Preemptive
    3C/3D shows a 6-card or longer suit and a weak hand.

There are four options for 3H and 3S responses to 1NT opening bids.

  • Slam Try
    3H/3S shows a good 6-card or longer suit, and promises slam interest.
  • Game Forcing
    3H/3S shows a good 5-card or longer suit, and is forcing to game.
  • Game Invitation
    3H/3S shows a 6-card or longer suit and a game invitational hand.
  • Preemptive
    3H/3S shows a 6-card or longer suit and a weak hand.
Preconfigured in standard bid convention sets (SAYC etc.).


2NT Rebids by Opener.

After 1 over 1 there are two options.

  • 18+ HCP and game forcing.
  • 17-18 HCP and non forcing.


After 2 over 1 there are two options.

  • 15-18 HCP and game forcing.
  • 12-14 HCP and non forcing.
Preconfigured in standard bid convention sets (SAYC etc.).


Overcalls

One level overcalls are made with a 5-card or longer suit and assumes 10 - 16 HCP. Non jump two level overcalls are made with a good 5-card or longer suit and assumes 12 - 16 HCP. With more HCP you should bid takeout double.
